What is a SOC?

A Security Operations Center (SOC) is a centralized function or team within an organization responsible for continuously monitoring, detecting, analyzing, and responding to cyber threats and incidents to improve the organization's overall security posture.

A SOC operates based on three key components: people, processes, and technology — working together to safeguard an organization's assets 24/7.

People, Processes & Technology

Effective SOC operations rely on the combination of trained personnel, repeatable processes (playbooks, runbooks, escalation paths) and technology (SIEM, EDR, network sensors, ticketing, threat intelligence).

Key Functions of a SOC

The responsibilities of a SOC are broad and generally fall into the following critical areas:

Continuous Monitoring

The SOC team constantly monitors the entire IT infrastructure — including networks, servers, endpoints, applications, and databases — for any suspicious activity or signs of a security breach. This often involves collecting and analyzing data from various sources using tools like a Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) system.

Preventative Maintenance & Planning

Proactive measures are taken to reduce the organization's attack surface. This includes:

  • Maintaining an inventory of all assets.
  • Applying security patches and updates.
  • Configuring security tools and firewalls.
  • Developing and regularly testing an Incident Response Plan.

Threat Detection and Analysis

When an alert is triggered, analysts investigate it to determine if it is a false positive or a legitimate security incident. They use threat intelligence — information about new vulnerabilities, attack vectors, and attacker motives — to understand the threat's nature, scope, and severity.

Incident Response

As the "first responders" to a confirmed security incident, the SOC team's actions are critical. They must act quickly to:

  • Contain the threat (e.g., isolating affected systems).
  • Mitigate the damage (e.g., removing malware).
  • Eradicate the cause of the incident.

Recovery and Remediation

After an attack is contained, the SOC is responsible for restoring systems, recovering compromised data, and returning the company to its original, secure state. They may also conduct a root cause investigation to prevent recurrence.

Compliance Management

The SOC ensures that the organization's security practices, processes, and tools comply with internal policies, industry standards, and government regulations (like GDPR or HIPAA).

Key Roles within a SOC Team

The structure of a SOC team varies by organization size, but common roles often include:

🟢 Tier 1 – Alert Monitoring & Triage

Focus: Detect → Validate → Escalate

Responsibilities:

  • Continuously monitor SIEM alerts (Splunk, QRadar, Sentinel, etc.).
  • Perform initial triage: classify alerts, filter false positives.
  • Collect basic evidence (source IP, event time, affected host/user).
  • Escalate true positives to Tier 2 with context.
  • Track incidents in ticketing systems (ServiceNow, JIRA).
  • Follow playbooks for common alerts (e.g., brute force, phishing).

Skill level: Junior to intermediate analysts — Example titles: SOC Analyst I, Security Monitoring Analyst

🟡 Tier 2 – Incident Responder

Focus: Investigate → Contain → Remediate

Responsibilities:

  • Deep-dive investigation of incidents escalated by Tier 1.
  • Execute containment actions (isolate host, disable account, block IP/domain).
  • Perform forensic triage (collect volatile data, run scripts).
  • Correlate multiple alerts to identify attack patterns.
  • Work with IT and business units for remediation.
  • Tune detection rules to reduce false positives.

Skill level: Intermediate to senior analyst — Example titles: SOC Analyst II, Incident Responder, Blue Team Engineer

🔴 Tier 3 – Threat Hunter / Advanced Analyst / Forensics

Focus: Proactive Detection → Threat Intelligence → Continuous Improvement

Responsibilities:

  • Conduct proactive Threat Hunting (searching for undetected threats using hypotheses, IOCs, and behavioral patterns).
  • Perform malware reverse engineering and deep forensics.
  • Develop and improve detection content (custom correlation searches, YARA rules).
  • Integrate and analyze Threat Intelligence (MISP, OpenCTI, etc.).
  • Automate and orchestrate responses via SOAR scripts (Python, PowerShell).
  • Tune SIEM and EDR to detect new TTPs (MITRE ATT&CK alignment).
  • Lead root cause analysis and lessons learned after incidents.
  • Mentor Tier 1 and 2 analysts.

Skill level: Senior-level / specialized experts — Example titles: Tier 3 SOC Analyst, Threat Hunter, DFIR Specialist, SOC Engineer

SOC Manager

Oversees the entire SOC operation, managing personnel, developing strategy, coordinating with other business units, and reporting to senior leadership.

Tools & Integrations

Common SOC tooling includes SIEMs for centralized logging and correlation; EDRs for endpoint detection and response; network sensors for traffic analysis; SOAR platforms for automation; ticketing systems for tracking; and threat intel platforms for context.

Operational Notes & Best Practices

  • Maintain and update playbooks and runbooks regularly.
  • Keep an accurate inventory of assets and exposures.
  • Patch and configure systems proactively to reduce attack surface.
  • Run tabletop exercises and IR drills to validate readiness.
  • Measure SOC KPIs: mean time to detect (MTTD), mean time to respond (MTTR), false positive rate, and coverage of critical assets.
  • Integrate threat intelligence to keep detection tuned for emerging TTPs.
