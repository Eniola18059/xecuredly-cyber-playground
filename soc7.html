Lesson 6: Identity & Access Management (IAM)

üîë What is IAM?

Identity and Access Management (IAM) ensures the right individuals and job roles in an organization can access the tools and data they need. It manages not only people, but also software, machines, IoT devices, and applications.

IAM allows organizations to manage users, groups, and permissions without having to log into every system separately. With IAM, you can decide who can do what within a system.

üÜî What is an Identity?

A digital identity is a collection of unique identifiers that represent a human, system, or device in a computer network. Examples include:

  • Email addresses
  • Login credentials (username & password)
  • Government IDs
  • IP addresses and MAC addresses

Digital identities are essential for authentication, authorization, communication, and transactions.

üîê Authentication

Authentication verifies who you are. Without it, systems cannot ensure that access requests are genuine. Methods include:

  • Credentials Verification ‚Äì username and password
  • Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) ‚Äì something you know (password), something you have (phone), something you are (biometrics)
  • Biometric Authentication ‚Äì fingerprint, iris, face recognition

Authorization defines what you can do once authenticated. Examples include:

  • Access Control Lists (ACLs) ‚Äì permissions for files/resources
  • Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) ‚Äì permissions assigned based on job roles
  • Principle of Least Privilege ‚Äì users only get the minimum required access

üõ°Ô∏è How IAM Works

IAM systems perform two main tasks:

  1. Authentication: Verifying that the user/device is who they say they are.
  2. Authorization: Granting the correct level of access (e.g., viewer, editor, admin).

Modern IAM solutions use cloud identity tools that are more secure and flexible than simple passwords.

üîí Password Protection

Password security is critical to IAM:

  • Enforce strong, complex passwords
  • Use MFA to strengthen login security
  • Change passwords regularly
  • Use Password Managers
  • Avoid password reuse across systems

üë• Identity Theft

Identity theft occurs when attackers steal personal information (e.g., SSN, credit card numbers) to impersonate someone and commit fraud. Common methods include:

  • Oversharing personal data on social media
  • Geo-tagging photos
  • Phishing and fake login sites
