Lesson 3: Cryptography

üîê Introduction

Cryptography is the science of protecting information by transforming it into unreadable formats for unauthorized users. It ensures confidentiality, integrity, and authentication in modern digital systems.

‚ö†Ô∏è Threats

Cyber threats include viruses, data breaches, denial of service, and eavesdropping. Cryptography provides a shield against these by encoding data.

üéØ Security Goals

  • Confidentiality: Only intended parties can read the data.
  • Integrity: Ensures data is not altered.
  • Authentication: Confirms the identity of users.
  • Non-repudiation: Prevents denial of actions by a sender.

üîë Encryption & Decryption

Encryption converts plaintext into ciphertext. Decryption transforms it back. Together, they protect data during storage and transmission.

üõ† Types of Cryptography

Symmetric Key Cryptography: Uses a single key for both encryption and decryption. Examples: DES, AES.

Asymmetric Key Cryptography: Uses two keys (public and private). Example: RSA.

Hash Functions: Irreversible, one-way algorithms. Used in password storage and blockchain.

üîÑ Ciphers

Ciphers are algorithms for encryption/decryption. Examples include substitution ciphers (replace characters) and transposition ciphers (rearrange characters).

üèõ Public Key Infrastructure (PKI)

PKI issues digital certificates, manages public/private keys, and ensures secure communication in systems like banking and e-commerce.

üí• Attacks on Cryptosystems

  • Passive Attacks: Eavesdropping without altering data.
  • Active Attacks: Modify or disrupt communication.
  • Ciphertext Only Attack: Attacker only has encrypted text.
  • Brute Force Attack: Trying all possible keys until success.

üìå Cryptography in Cybersecurity

Used in ATM cards, passwords, and e-commerce. Ensures secure digital communication and trust between systems.
