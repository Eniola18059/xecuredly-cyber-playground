2.1 The Cybersecurity Operations Cycle (The SOC Workflow)

This chapter focuses on the day-to-day work of the Security Operations Center, detailing the cycle of threat management and the essential technology that powers it.

The SOC's work is a continuous cycle designed to improve security over time. The cycle can be broken into three main phases: Preparation & Prevention (Proactive), Detection & Analysis (Monitoring), and Response & Recovery (Reactive).

1) Preparation and Prevention (Proactive)

  • Vulnerability Management: Routine scans to identify weaknesses; prioritize patching and remediation.
  • Threat Intelligence (ThreatFeed): Collect and analyze data on new attack trends, malware signatures, and attacker TTPs to update defenses before attacks occur.
  • Maintenance: Ensure security tools (firewalls, EDR, SIEM) are updated, configured, and ingesting logs correctly.
  • Policy & Control Review: Test and verify that security policies (access control, encryption) are enforced.

2) Detection and Analysis (Monitoring)

  • Log Aggregation & Correlation: Collect events in the SIEM and correlate unrelated events to spot attack patterns.
  • Alert Triage & Prioritization: Tier 1 validates alerts, filters false positives, assigns severity and context.
  • Deep Investigation: Tier 2/3 analyze confirmed incidents to determine scope, root cause, and attacker objectives.

3) Response and Recovery (Reactive)

  • Containment: Isolate systems, segment networks, revoke credentials to stop spread — time-critical.
  • Eradication: Remove threats (delete malware, reimage systems, remove persistence).
  • Recovery & Remediation: Restore operations from clean backups and apply fixes/config changes to close exploited gaps.

4) Post-Incident Activities (Learning)

This reactive phase feeds back into Preparation & Prevention.

  • Documentation & Reporting: Record timeline, IoCs, actions taken and evidence.
  • Lessons Learned Review: Blameless post-mortem to identify detection/process gaps.
  • Security Control Enhancement: Create new detection rules, recommend patches or architecture changes.

2.2 Understanding Cyber Threats, IoCs, and Attack Methodology

To execute the workflow, analysts must understand the adversary and the attack lifecycle.

Define Cyber Threats & Attacks

Cyber Threat: Potential danger that could exploit a vulnerability. Cyber Attack: Deliberate execution of a threat to damage, disrupt, or gain unauthorized access.

Network, Host, and Application-Level Attacks

1. Network-Level Attacks 🌐

Targets infrastructure (OSI Layers 3–4):

  • DDoS: Overwhelm services (SYN flood, UDP flood, amplification like DNS/NTP).
  • MiTM: Intercept or alter communications.

2. Host-Level Attacks 💻

  • Malware & Ransomware: Encrypt or control endpoints.
  • Privilege Escalation: Gain elevated permissions on a host.

3. Application-Level Attacks ⚙️

  • SQL Injection (SQLi): Inject SQL to manipulate databases.
  • XSS: Inject client-side scripts into pages viewed by users.
  • Zero-Day Exploits: Use unknown vendor vulnerabilities.
  • App-level DDoS: HTTP GET/POST floods, Slowloris, recursive request attacks.

Indicators of Compromise (IoCs)

IoCs are forensic artifacts that indicate a breach. They guide containment and detection updates.

2.4 Threat Intelligence (TI) and Threat Hunting

2.4.1 Defining Threat Intelligence (TI)

Definition: Context-rich, processed information about adversaries, TTPs and capabilities used to inform decisions.

Intelligence Cycle: Direction → Collection → Processing → Analysis → Dissemination.

Types of Intelligence:

  • Strategic: Executive-level risks and trends.
  • Tactical: TTPs and techniques for defenders.
  • Operational: Imminent or ongoing campaign details.
  • Technical: Raw IoCs (IPs, hashes, domains).

2.4.2 The Role of Threat Hunting

Definition: Proactive search for undetected threats assuming a breach may exist.

Hunting Methodology:

  • Hypothesis generation based on TI.
  • Investigation/data mining via SIEM/EDR.
  • Discovery of new IoCs/TTPs.
  • Create new detections and close the loop back to prevention.

2.5 SOC Team Structure and Tiers

An effective SOC separates responsibilities across tiers to manage volume and complexity.

Tier 1: Alert Triage & Monitoring

Focus: Continuous SIEM monitoring. Tasks: Validate alerts, gather initial data, escalate. Skills: SIEM familiarity, basic incident handling.

Tier 2: Incident Response & Deep Investigation

Focus: Handle escalations. Tasks: Full IR procedures, scope compromise, basic forensics. Skills: Malware analysis, scripting, networking.

Tier 3: Threat Hunting & Engineering

Focus: Proactive detection and tool engineering. Tasks: Hunting, custom correlation rules, tool tuning. Skills: Advanced scripting, reverse engineering.

Core Principles: CIA, PoLP, Defense-in-Depth

Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability (CIA)

Confidentiality: Prevent unauthorized disclosure (encryption, ACLs, DLP).
Integrity: Ensure data is accurate and unmodified (hashing, change management).
Availability: Ensure authorized access (redundancy, patching, failover).

Principle of Least Privilege (PoLP)

Grant only the minimum permissions required. PoLP limits lateral movement and reduces impact of credential compromise.

Defense-in-Depth

Layer multiple independent controls so that if one fails the others provide detection or prevention. Examples: firewall + EDR + network segmentation + monitoring.
